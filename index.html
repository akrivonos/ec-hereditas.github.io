European Center for Vernacular Spiritual Heritage Studies — “Hereditas”

We investigate Europe’s folk spiritual cultures — cosmologies, rituals, myths, healing, demonology, and material forms of devotion — with a comparative ethnological approach anchored in the Carpathian region.

About

EC Hereditas (short for European Center for Vernacular Spiritual Heritage Studies) is a virtual, research‑driven organization created by scholars of Hutsul demonology, folk beliefs and folk magic. We publish articles, curate open corpora, and carry out ethnographic expeditions beginning in the Carpathians.

Mission

To document, analyze, and compare Europe’s vernacular spiritual heritage, and to support its preservation as intangible cultural heritage.

Approach

We bridge comparative ethnology, anthropology of religion, folklore studies, and field research, grounded in both the theoretical and empirical achievements of world anthropology.

Research

The Carpathian region serves as our starting field laboratory, offering a lens for broader European comparison.

1. Cosmologies and Everyday Ontologies

  • Ideas of soul, destiny, and individual “gift” or “power.”
  • Categories of spirits, saints, and “unclean” beings; liminal zones — roads, bridges, thresholds, boundary stones.
  • Comparative analysis of confessional and regional ontologies: Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox, Greek‑Catholic, Jewish, and Muslim environments and their syncretic intersections.

2. Vernacular Rites and Ritual Cycles

  • Calendar rites: Christmas divinations, New‑Year fortune‑telling, Epiphany blessings, spring purification and fertility rituals, midsummer fires, harvest festivals, ancestor commemorations.
  • Life‑cycle rites: birth and naming, baptismal and protective rituals for infants, initiation and marriage ceremonies, funerary and memorial customs.
  • Domestic and local rituals: household blessings, consecration of tools, field and livestock rites, processions, and village festivals.
  • Interpretation through classic models of transition and liminality (van Gennep, Turner) and regional “calendars of sanctity.”

3. Magic, Healing, and Protective Practices

  • Charms, spells, and apocryphal prayers for healing, protection, and success.
  • Social roles of healers, midwives, and charmers; ritual techniques such as “taking away milk,” “extinguishing fire,” or curing the “evil eye.”
  • Symbolic media of power — herbs, threads, metals, water, fire, eggs, stones.
  • Interfaces of popular religion, folk medicine, and ecological symbolism.

4. Demonology and “Extraordinary” Beings or People

  • Local spirit‑types: witches, werewolves, revenants, house spirits, forest and water entities.
  • Social meanings of fear, transgression, and spiritual danger.
  • Gendered and status‑based aspects of magical authority and stigma.

5. Altered States and Mediumship

  • Dreams, trances, possessions, prophetic inspiration, ecstatic speech.
  • Continuities of shamanic and visionary techniques within European traditions.
  • Phenomenological, psychological, and neuro‑anthropological approaches to embodied religiosity.

6. Folklore, Mythology, and Narrative Worlds

  • Magical fairy tales, local legends, belief stories, myths, and memorates.
  • Comparative study of motifs and archetypes across European mythological systems.
  • Storytelling as ritual performance — words as acts of creation and protection.
  • Documentation of regional storytellers, oral archives, and performative settings.

7. Songs, Ritual Poetry, and Performance

  • Carols, laments, healing songs, ritual incantations, and processional chants.
  • Rhythm, formula, and voice as vehicles of collective emotion and sacred agency.
  • Ritual scenography: spatial arrangement, costume, gesture, and sacred objects.

8. Material Expressions of Spirituality

  • Icons, roadside crosses, chapels, sacred trees and springs, talismans.
  • Craft practices linked to ritual life — candle‑making, weaving, wood‑carving, metalwork.
  • Symbolism of materials and the relation between maker, object, and rite.

Fieldwork and Methodology

Our research combines archival study, ethnographic fieldwork, and digital documentation. Expeditions across Europe — beginning in the Carpathian highlands — record oral traditions, rituals, and material heritage through interviews, photography, and audio‑visual media.

Goals

  • Build a comparative atlas of European vernacular spirituality.
  • Create an open digital corpus of charms, prayers, narratives, and songs.
  • Advance interdisciplinary collaboration in ethnology, folklore, and religious anthropology.
  • Support the preservation and revitalization of Europe’s intangible spiritual heritage.
